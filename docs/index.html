The Hidden Layers of Sesame Allergies

By Russell Goldenberg

cue: walks in

And Michelle McGhee

I don’t have food allergies. I never gave them much thought, eating whatever I wanted.

cue: walks to center, infinite walking with food flying by

Then I had my daughter.

cue: hospital appears, Russell walks hospital, arms slide out from hospital doors (which open), walks back to other side, hospital fades out

After introducing solid foods at six months, I learned she was allergic to peanuts.

cue: Russell holds baby in air, reuse flying foods (or baby in high chair). Anthropomorphic mean peanut walks in, dancing or something

She got tested: tree nuts and sesame were added to the list.

cue: walk to hospital which reappears, arms reach out and stab her with needle, other allergens appear and dance with peanut
deep dive

Technically the tree nuts she is allergic to are cashews and pistachios. And technically, they aren’t even nuts, but seeds! Although tree nuts are lumped into a single category, they are made up of cashews, pistachios, walnuts, ...add rest.

I knew peanuts and tree nuts were common, but sesame surprised me. It was time to learn about food allergies.

cue: everything disappears except Russell

scene: education

This is Dr. Ruchi Gupta. She literally wrote the book on food allergies. Here is what I learned:

cue: Gupta writing on chalkboard, Russell walks to new spot

About 32 million Americans have food allergies, or 1 in 10 adults and 1 in 13 children.

cue: zoomed in chalkboard appears full screen

Food allergies are on the rise in recent decades. Sesame is now the ninth top allergen in the US, yet not required to be labeled by law under FALCPA until 2023.

cue: % from 2019 survey in adults ranking top eight by percent

FALCPA stands for the Food Allergen Labeling And Consumer Protection Act of 2004.

cue: the FDA “requires that the label of a food that contains an ingredient that is or contains protein from a ‘major food allergen’ declare the presence of the allergen in the manner described by the law.”

FALCPA is a great start, but it can be improved. For example, precautionary allergen labeling (PAL), like “may contain peanuts,” is unregulated and open to consumer interpretation.

cue: survey results visualize + examples

scene: experiment

Michelle has food allergies too. We shared our experiences and talked about the complexities of food labeling.

cue: chalkboard goes away, then everything else, Michelle walks in next to Russell, watercooler appears

Here is how sesame can end up in a product with varying degrees of transparency.

cue: Russell and Michelle walk to side, Michelle holds cracker box, watercooler disappears

Most obviously, sesame can be in the product ingredients with the common name.

cue: giant label appears (some sort of diagram link to box to show it is enhanced? or animates from box origin), highlight “sesame seeds”

Less obviously, sesame can be referred to by other names.

cue: pans to “tahini”

Sesame can be hidden in catch-all ingredients like “spices” and “natural flavors”

cue: pans again and highlights those.

Most opaquely, sesame can be accidentally included by cross-contact via shared equipment (where two different products are produced on the same line) or in a shared facility.

cue: label goes away, assembly line appears, label toggling between two cracker types on machine, Michelle stops holding box
deep dive

A Canadian study found that 17% of accidental exposures came from unlabeled ingredients

Whew, that is a lot to consider! We decided to run an experiment to reveal how the hidden layers of labeling affect our everyday lives.

cue: machine fades out, Michelle and Russell now wearing goggles, one holding test tubes, one holding calculator

scene: store

A typical and time-consuming activity for my daughter and me is to shop for groceries. We focused on crackers; a snacking staple that can often have sesame seeds.

cue: Michelle walks off screen, Daughter walks on screen next to Russell.

We picked the 200 most popular crackers from two massive grocery chains, Albertsons (conventional) and Whole Foods (natural).

cue: pile of crackers appears

How much clarity and confidence can we have when evaluating each cracker for sesame?

cue: pile of crackers get checks or Xs (or red/green)

We contacted all 49 manufacturers to get details about their approaches to labeling and manufacturing practices. The results were grouped into three tiers of clarity.

cue: crackers disappear, Russell and Daughter infinite walk cycle through shelves of crackers that appear
deep dive

We first tried looking at a manufacturer's website, some have great FAQ pages. Then we tried emailing or filling out a contact form. Lastly we tried calling their customer support.

Tier 1: We can confidently evaluate these crackers in the store because sesame is labeled or it is made in an allergen-free facility. X% of the X crackers we looked at are in this tier.

cue: crackers stop moving, full 200 appear, just tier one appears
deep dive

It is recommended to always read a label every time you purchase a product because ingredients can change! Most mainstream products still don’t consider sesame a top allergen, so we didn’t encounter any that claimed to be made in a sesame-free facility, though they do exist.

Tier 2: After learning about a manufacturer’s process, we are confident on how to read their label to consume or avoid their crackers. X% of the X crackers we looked at are in this tier..

cue: tier one disappears, tier two appears
deep dive

Manufacturers can change up their processing or facilities so it is good to check in regularly.

Tier 3: We got unclear or vague answers, or no response at all about the manufacturer’s processing practices. We should avoid these crackers no matter what the label might say. X% of the X crackers we looked at are in this tier.

cue: tier two disappears, tier three appears

With so many hidden risks and variability, it's no wonder some people just choose to avoid products altogether that aren't explicitly made in allergen-free facilities.
